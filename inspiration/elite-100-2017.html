Strategies for success:

I use IXL in my Honors Algebra class as a replacement for most in-class worksheets and homework, as our school has a 1-1 iPad initiative. After teaching a new concept and working through some sample problems together as a class, I assign related lessons on IXL for my students to complete in class. Anything that's not finished in class becomes homework. During class, I encourage students to ask me or a peer for help if they are unsure about a certain lesson, but the built-in explanations allow them to get help even when they're home finishing up an assignment. Parents love it, too, as they can see in the explanation what to do as a quick refresher, and are better able to help their children. Since I started using IXL, I have not had a single student fail to pass the Pennsylvania Keystone Exam at the end of the year, and most receive scores in the advanced range! I think IXL is great for remediation and enrichment as it really fosters an environment of customization in how quickly they progress through the lessons.

Favorite teacher tools:

I love that IXL has replaced the need for most paper worksheets. Also, I love that students get instant feedback on how they are doing and explanations of what they did incorrectly before they move on. When I used worksheets as homework, students would sometimes complete an entire assignment incorrectly—now they can't do that, as IXL doesn't allow their SmartScore to increase unless they are getting problems correct. For grading purposes, I use the Score Grid. For monitoring progress in class, I love the Real-Time Center. It allows me to see who's struggling and not asking for assistance. By the end of the first month of school, my students know to ask for help because they know I can see how they're doing, and that I'll be visiting them if they don't come to me first. I also use a variety of the reports at parent-teacher conferences, depending on what I need to discuss with each individual parent.

Making it fun:

I use competition in my class a lot, and we openly share how much the students have done on IXL on a weekly basis. In the honors setting, the competitive spirit is high and some kids really like trying to be the one with the most topics mastered or the highest SmartScore gained in a week. Even those who don't thrive on competition, the open sharing of how much everyone has done allows them to see how they're keeping pace with their peers. The leaderboard changes so much that I generally have a different top and bottom [student] every time I post an update.

Strategies for success:

My students have access to 1-1 devices within my numeracy block, which allows me to use the program as part of my rotations. Students are able to work independently, in pairs, or in small guided groups with my guidance. My students also use it as part of their nightly homework. I encourage my students to continually challenge themselves to accomplish small goals on a regular basis, often within small sections of the curriculum. As a teacher, I love that all students are able to get a daily sense of achievement regardless of their prior knowledge and understandings. Within my class cohort, I have students working between years 3-9 depending on their confidence—meaning that differentiation is made so much easier for me, and the results are in the data on a yearly basis. When I first introduced IXL to my students 3 years ago, they had no confidence in their own abilities, with numeracy [being] their least favourite curriculum area. Now students are begging to do numeracy! I attribute a huge portion of this success to the regular use of IXL.

Strategies for success:

Teaching three grade levels requires not only attention to differentiation, but also a myriad of standards to navigate. With IXL, I can scaffold a lesson where I start with whole-group instruction on a skill that spans all three grade levels, give the opportunity for partner-work as a second look at the concept, and then have them "show what they know" on IXL. Also, being able to search a skill in the search box makes it very efficient for me to pinpoint where each grade level will find their specific location for practice. Once they are in the final phase of the lesson ("show what you know"), the students know that if they get a question wrong, they should carefully read the explanation and see if they can independently discover their own mistake. If they get another one wrong, they are required to have a "mini workshop" with myself or another student that has already completed that skill. This is usually an upper level student being a guide on the side for a lower level student. The philosophy is that if the older student can teach it, they are reinforcing their understanding of the concept.

Favorite teacher tools:

The students and I both appreciate the Real-Time Center in the Analytics tab. All green in the Real-Time Center shows me that the lesson was effective. When students have two red blocks, it instantly allows me to intercept their lack of understanding and give them extra instruction until they are back on track.

Making it fun:

The students absolutely LOVE working on IXL. I think that they connect their learning routine to a sense of accomplishment, and it's gratifying. They know that if they pay attention during the group lesson that they are accountable for the information on IXL. Throughout the year when we give STAR tests, the students can see that their grade equivalency increases if they are focused. IXL is one of the main vehicles of growth and they get excited as they strive for more knowledge!

Strategies for success:

IXL is a fantastic tool for reinforcing classroom learning. After teaching a lesson, I assign objectives that match the concept and students complete them for homework to reinforce the concept taught in class. The goal is for them to reach a SmartScore between 50 and 60 by the following class day. This gives them hands-on experience with the concept and a foundation that I can build on the following day. Every new concept is reinforced this way.

Favorite teacher tools:

The feature I most appreciate is the immediate feedback! I've noticed that students are reading this text carefully and truly working to understand the concept. Not only have their math skills and math confidence improved, but I have also noticed that students are becoming better math readers. In Analytics, I use the Scores report to check for daily completion of their homework. I also quickly hover on top of SmartScores across the assigned objective. This gives me a quick read of how many problems each student attempted and how much time was spent on an objective. I can quickly tell whether the class or individuals struggled with a concept. The ability to go back and see missed problems in the Questions Log report is an essential tool I use when working one-on-one with a struggling student. In class, I use the Real-Time report to monitor students. The class rule is if you turn red, you must bring me your work before you hit submit on the next problem. I often project this page [on the board] as I work around the room with individuals and groups. It becomes a fun competition and students work to [avoid] the “danger zone” by asking each other and me for help.

Making it fun:

To further their learning and develop long-term understanding, students are assigned what we call “IXL 90's.” Within a week after a concept is introduced, they must reach a SmartScore of 90. They have a week to go back to the previous concepts taught and chew through the levels to attain an adequate level of mastery. Grades are assigned out of 90 so there is room for high achievers to get bonus points by going above 90. Special recognition is given to all students who meet all weekly goals, and those that regularly reach 100 are given elite status.
