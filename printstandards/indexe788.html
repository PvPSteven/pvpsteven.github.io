Skills available for New Hampshire third-grade science standards

Standards are in bold, followed by a list of the IXL science skills that are aligned to that standards. Students can practice these skills online at www.ixl.com.

Standards: New Hampshire Grade Level and Grade Span Expectations: Physical Science

Physical Science

  • PS1 All living and nonliving things are composed of matter having characteristic properties that distinguish one substance from another (independent of size/amount of substance).

    • 1 Composition

      • S:PS1:4:1.1 Explain that materials may be composed of parts that are too small to be seen without magnification.

        • Interpret ball-and-stick models (5-F.1)

      • S:PS1:4:1.2 Use measures of weight (data) to demonstrate that the whole equals the sum of its parts.

    • 2 Properties

      • S:PS1:4:2.1 Recognize that substances can be classified by observable properties.

        • Identify properties of an object (3-A.1)
        • Compare properties of objects (3-A.2)
        • Compare properties of materials (3-A.3)
        • Identify materials in objects (3-A.4)
        • Identify multiple materials in objects (3-A.5)
        • Compare properties of objects (4-A.1)
        • Compare properties of materials (4-A.2)

      • S:PS1:4:2.2 Explain that some materials can exist in different states; and describe the distinct physical properties of each state of matter.

        • Classify matter as solid, liquid, or gas (3-B.1)
        • Identify solids, liquids, and gases (3-B.2)
        • Sort solids, liquids, and gases (3-B.3)
        • Identify and sort solids, liquids, and gases (4-C.1)

      • S:PS1:4:2.3 Explain how some materials, such as water, can change from one state to another by heating or cooling.

        • Change-of-state diagrams: solid, liquid, and gas (3-C.1)
        • Heating, cooling, and changes of state (3-C.2)
        • Change-of-state diagrams: solid, liquid, and gas (4-C.2)
        • Heating, cooling, and changes of state (4-C.3)

      • S:PS1:4:2.4 Make a prediction about what might happen to the state of common materials when heated or cooled; or categorize materials as solid, liquid, or gas.

        • Classify matter as solid, liquid, or gas (3-B.1)
        • Identify solids, liquids, and gases (3-B.2)
        • Sort solids, liquids, and gases (3-B.3)
        • Change-of-state diagrams: solid, liquid, and gas (3-C.1)
        • Heating, cooling, and changes of state (3-C.2)
        • Identify and sort solids, liquids, and gases (4-C.1)
        • Change-of-state diagrams: solid, liquid, and gas (4-C.2)
        • Heating, cooling, and changes of state (4-C.3)

      • S:PS1:4:2.5 Collect and organize data about physical properties in order to classify objects or draw conclusions about objects and their characteristic properties (e.g., temperature, color, size, shape, weight, texture, flexibility).

        • Identify properties of an object (3-A.1)
        • Compare properties of objects (3-A.2)
        • Compare properties of materials (3-A.3)
        • Identify materials in objects (3-A.4)
        • Identify multiple materials in objects (3-A.5)
        • Classify matter as solid, liquid, or gas (3-B.1)
        • Identify solids, liquids, and gases (3-B.2)
        • Sort solids, liquids, and gases (3-B.3)
        • Classify rocks and minerals by color, shape, and texture (3-Q.1)
        • Compare properties of objects (4-A.1)
        • Compare properties of materials (4-A.2)
        • Calculate density (4-B.1)
        • Identify and sort solids, liquids, and gases (4-C.1)

  • PS2 Energy is necessary for change to occur in matter. Energy can be stored, transferred and transformed, but cannot be destroyed.

    • 1 Change

      • S:PS2:4:1.1 Recognize that energy has the ability to create change.

    • 2 Conservation

    • 3 Energy

      • S:PS2:4:3.1 Identify the various forms of energy, such as electrical, light, heat, sound.

      • S:PS2:4:3.2 Recognize that electricity in circuits can produce light, heat, sound, and magnetic effects.

      • S:PS2:4:3.3 Identify and describe the organization of a simple circuit.

      • S:PS2:4:3.4 Differentiate between objects and materials that conduct electricity and those that are insulators of electricity.

      • S:PS2:4:3.5 Explain that light travels in a straight line until it strikes an object; and describe how it can be reflected by a mirror, bent by a lens, or absorbed by the object.

      • S:PS2:4:3.6 Given a specific example or illustration (e.g., simple closed circuit, rubbing hands together) predict the observable effects of energy (i.e., the bulb lights, a bell rings, hands warm up). A test item may ask, "What will happen when?"

      • S:PS2:4:3.7 Use observations of light in relation to other objects/substances to describe the properties of light (i.e., can be reflected, refracted, or absorbed).

      • S:PS2:4:3.8 Experiment, observe, or predict how heat might move from one object to another.

        • Predict heat flow (3-D.1)
        • Predict temperature changes (3-D.2)
        • How is temperature related to thermal energy? (3-D.3)
        • Predict heat flow (4-D.1)
        • Predict temperature changes (4-D.2)
        • How is temperature related to thermal energy? (4-D.3)

  • PS3 The motion of an object is affected by force.

    • 1 Forces

      • S:PS3:4:1.1 Recognize that magnets attract certain kinds of other materials; and classify objects by those magnets will attract and those they will not.

        • Solve problems using magnets (3-V.1)

      • S:PS3:4:1.2 Recognize that magnets attract and repel each other.

        • Identify magnets that attract or repel (3-I.1)
        • Compare strengths of magnetic forces (3-I.3)
        • Identify magnets that attract or repel (4-I.1)
        • Compare strengths of magnetic forces (4-I.3)

      • S:PS3:4:1.3 Explain that electrically charged material pulls on all other materials and can attract or repel other charged materials.

        • Introduction to static electricity and charged objects (3-H.1)
        • Introduction to static electricity and charged objects (4-H.1)

      • S:PS3:4:1.4 Recognize that the Earth's gravitational force pulls any object toward it.

      • S:PS3:4:1.5 Use observations of magnets in relation to other objects to describe the properties of magnetism (i.e., attract or repel certain objects or has no effect).

        • Identify magnets that attract or repel (3-I.1)
        • Compare strengths of magnetic forces (3-I.3)
        • Solve problems using magnets (3-V.1)
        • Identify magnets that attract or repel (4-I.1)
        • Compare strengths of magnetic forces (4-I.3)

    • 2 Motion

      • S:PS3:4:2.1 Use data to predict how a change in force (greater/less) might affect the position, direction of motion, or speed of an object (e.g., ramps and balls).

        • Identify pushes and pulls (3-G.1)
        • How do mass and force affect motion? (3-G.3)
        • Identify directions of forces (4-G.1)
        • How does mass affect force and acceleration? (4-G.3)

  • PS4 The growth of scientific knowledge in Physical Science has been advanced through the development of technology and is used (alone or in combination with other sciences) to identify, understand and solve local and global issues.

    • 1 Design Technology

      • S:PS4:4:1.1 Understand that materials are used in certain products based on their properties, such as strength and flexibility.

      • S:PS4:4:1.2 Recognize that products are made using a combination of technologies, such as how an escalator uses both a pulley system and an electrical motor.

    • 2 Tools

      • S:PS4:4:2.1 Demonstrate how to use tools, such as magnifiers, scales, balances, rulers, and thermometers to gather data and extend the senses.

        • Read a thermometer (3-S.1)
        • Compare temperatures on thermometers (3-S.2)
        • Collect and graph temperature data (3-S.3)
        • Read a thermometer (4-U.1)
        • Compare temperatures on thermometers (4-U.2)
        • Collect and graph temperature data (4-U.3)

      • S:PS4:4:2.2 Describe how some tools can be used to modify natural materials by processes such as separating, shaping, and joining, to produce new materials.

    • 3 Social Issues (Local and Global), Energy, Power, and Transportation, Manufacturing

      • S:PS4:4:3.1 Give examples of transportation systems used in New Hampshire, such as buses, trains, cars, and bicycles; and describe the sources of energy they use.

      • S:PS4:4:3.2 Explain that manufactured products are designed to solve a problem or meet a need.

      • S:PS4:4:3.3 Provide an example to illustrate that manufacturing involves changing natural materials into finished products; and explain that this results in the production of a large number of objects that look almost identical.

    • 4 Career Technical Education Connections

      • S:PS4:4:4.1 Identify some jobs/careers that require knowledge and use of physical science content and/or skills.
